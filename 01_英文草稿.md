# From Outcome Archives to Decision Traces: Multimodal Evidence Engineering, Target Auditing, and Failure Gating for Regulatory AI with Limited Authority

## A Retrospective Methods Study of Pennsylvania Oil and Gas Well-Plugging Review Records

## Abstract

### Background

As foundation models, multimodal systems, and AI agents enter real-world workflows, the role of data has expanded beyond pretraining and post-training material to include runtime state, organizational memory, and the basis for decisions. Many institutions do not lack operational outcomes, but they do lack replayable **decision traces**: who saw what at the time, which version of a rule applied, which options were tried, why a case was escalated or treated as an exception, and what evidence supported the action. Regulatory decisions are often case-specific and path-dependent, moving through application, discussion, supplemental submission, and final ruling. Structured databases, however, usually give priority to the current or final state. Original case files may preserve some fragments, but they do not organize decision-time state, reasons, sources, and later outcomes into one auditable unit. Historical case files are therefore not snapshots of the moment when a decision was made. If applications, correspondence, later work, approvals, and completion certificates are compressed into one table, a model can use future information to predict an outcome and can mistake selective labels for normative truth.

### Objective

Using Pennsylvania attainable-bottom review for oil and gas well plugging as a case study, this research examined whether partial decision traces could be reconstructed from outcome-oriented archives, how unrecoverable gaps limit model constructs, and how an agent architecture should capture future decision traces by design. Five specific questions addressed the evidence pipeline, grouped generalization, the distinction between difficulty and adequacy, the survival of checklist and documented non-routine difficulty (DN) constructs, and the reduction of system authority after failure gates.

### Methods

We defined a decision trace as including the case, index time, actor, observed state, request/action, attempted alternatives, source evidence, rule/version, decision/reason, uncertainty/override, and later outcome, while keeping the later outcome separate from the decision state. On this basis, we reconstructed and audited a research chain that included multimodal reading of mixed-format PDFs; file-page-original-text-visual-state provenance tuples; separate requested, approved, and certificate time tracks; numerical, evidentiary, and temporal safeguards; a quarantine queue; county-grouped cross-validation; label and censoring audits; version-controlled protocol freezing; independent blinded AI-agent reading; researcher adjudication; and failure gates. The source-file scope included 635 case folders containing approximately 1,700 PDFs. The frozen shortfall cohort contained 631 cases from 29 counties; the DN/Model A cohort contained 424 cases, and 423 cases had teacher scores available for Model B.

### Results

The multimodal pipeline produced 633 unique case-level records, 10,856 depth-evidence rows, and 436 quarantine records, but it could reconstruct only partial traces. In a fixed, targeted 40-case regression set, precision and recall were both 1.0 for three fields; this was not a corpus-wide accuracy estimate. In county-grouped evaluation, a profile that mixed request evidence with nongeographic well attributes such as TD, well age, casing, water, and coal had an AUC of 0.740±0.009. A geography profile containing only county and formation had an AUC of 0.625±0.006. This comparison was not a causal decomposition into an “effort axis” and a “geographic difficulty axis.” An early step-location AUC of 0.916 was reclassified as circular because a zero-parameter deepest-event rule recovered 79 of 103 labels. An 89-case censoring sensitivity analysis reduced shortfall-risk AUC from 0.757 to 0.742 and 0.689, ruling out an effort-adequacy interpretation.

Among 424 cases, 423 had at least one dated event, but explicit records of an “attempt followed by failure” appeared in only 6.1%, obstruction depth in 14.6%, and maximum reached depth in 18.9%. Nearly all detailed work-record flags were below 2.5%, and 93.15% of events had no tool token. Difficulty types were usually recorded about 3–74 times more often in application narratives than in the separate work-record layer. This pattern describes selective documentation, not actual effort. Internal project adjudication identified 112 of 424 cases as DN-positive. Model A was a DN-prescreen HGB classifier with an internal county-grouped AUC of 0.8366±0.1097 and was used only to order the human review queue. Model B was an HGB proxy regressor for a locked historical shortfall ensemble. In one five-fold county-grouped evaluation, it had a Spearman correlation of 0.418±0.047, exact-bin agreement of 35.5%, and within-one-bin agreement of 72.3%. It was used to select an empirical reference bin and indirectly affected the preliminary route through a later tail flag. The two models shared 17 request-time-compatible fields, but neither had approval authority.

The nominal primary G9 comparison was 7/13 versus 105/411, with a two-sided Fisher p=0.0477, but two prefrozen sensitivity conditions failed. The combined Path-B AUC was 0.6841, below the 0.764 threshold. Retrospective routing sent five of seven depth-related refusals to the fast lane. Accordingly, authority for approval/refusal, automatic routing based on historical outcome state, and adverse anomaly decisions was disabled.

### Conclusions

These results support a research discipline that shifts from “outcome prediction” to “decision-trace infrastructure,” not an automated approval system. The formal definition of attainable bottom requires DEP approval and is conditioned on **reasonable effort** to clean out to TD, not maximum or greatest effort. Existing archives support, at most, two evidence channels: a trace of effort evidence and a context of difficulty. The first records what was done and where that information came from; the second describes well and physical constraints. Neither automatically equals adequacy. Model A only allocates review attention, Model B only selects a coarse empirical reference, rules display explicit missing items, and a qualified reviewer verifies the facts under the applicable rule and remains responsible for the final judgment. The next stage requires complete traces to be recorded directly at request time, an independent domain-expert standard, external operator/time/template holdouts, and prospective human-system trials.

**Keywords:** regulatory artificial intelligence; decision traces; decision provenance; organizational memory; multimodal document understanding; multi-agent evidence engineering; selective labels; temporal leakage; construct validity; failure gating; oil and gas well plugging

## 1. Introduction

The data problem of foundation models is usually understood as the question of what material should be used for pretraining or post-training. Once agents enter contract review, customer service, compliance, and regulatory workflows, data must also represent runtime world state, organizational memory, precedent, and the chain of responsibility. A business table may record that a discount was 20%, while failing to preserve the policy version in force at the time, the person who approved it, the reason for the exception, the precedents that were compared, or the discussion that led to the decision. Human teams have often filled these gaps through personal experience and organizational memory. An agent that sees only the final value cannot replay the decision moment or distinguish a policy exception from a data error or an arbitrary departure.

We call this gap the **decision-trace deficit**. A decision trace is not a general log or a model explanation generated after the fact. It is a traceable unit that connects the state, actions, evidence, rules, reasons, and responsible actors at the time of a decision. An ideal trace contains at least the case/decision ID, index time, actors, observed state, request/action, attempted alternatives, source evidence, applicable rule/version, decision/reason, and uncertainty/override. A later outcome is linked separately and does not overwrite the decision state.

Many institutions do not completely “lack reasons.” Reasons may be scattered across PDFs, emails, meeting minutes, work logs, and personal memory. The problem with structured databases is that they usually prioritize the current or final state and lack a unified relationship among time, source, and reason. The central challenge in high-consequence document intelligence is therefore not whether a number can be extracted. It is when the number became knowable, what supports it, which track it belongs to, why it entered the decision, and what action the system may be authorized to take. Completion records may look rich in information but mix in future facts. Historical approvals may look like labels but reflect only the path that was observed and documented.

Pennsylvania oil and gas well-plugging records provide a representative setting. [25 Pa. Code §78.1](https://www.pacodeandbulletin.gov/display/pacode?d=reduce&file=%2Fsecure%2Fpacode%2Fdata%2F025%2Fchapter78%2FsubchapAtoc.html) defines attainable bottom as the depth approved by DEP that can be reached after **reasonable effort** has been made to clean out to total depth. The regulation does not use maximum effort or greatest effort. An applicant may ask to stop above TD because of a collapse, hard fill, casing failure, a coal seam, or another obstruction. Regulators need more than the isolated outcome of the final plugging depth. They need to know what had been done at the time of the request, when an attempt failed, what constraints were encountered, where the information came from, which rule applied at the time, and why the request was approved, returned for more information, or refused.

The project went through three corrections in problem definition. First, the early task of “locating which step became the final step within approved cases” was not the same as predicting a regulatory decision. It was later shown to be driven mainly by the label’s own definition as the deepest event. Second, case-level shortfall risk or difficulty was not the same as the adequacy of an operator’s effort. Third, outcome-state case files could not reliably reconstruct the information set available at request time. One important related correction was that the absence of a formal effort narrative in a document could not support an inference of “no effort.” The project therefore narrowed the construct to documented non-routine difficulty (DN).

This development shows that a model cannot uniquely reconstruct decision state from final state. A judgment process that the archive did not preserve cannot be recovered simply by adding features or increasing model complexity. This leads to the central claim of this paper:

> **Agent readiness depends on decision-trace readiness. Accuracy has meaning for high-consequence decisions only when measurement, time, construct, generalization, and decision authority are all valid at the same time.**

This paper makes five contributions. First, it clearly distinguishes an outcome archive, a structured state, and a decision trace, and it provides an operational trace schema. Second, it presents a multimodal evidence-engineering pipeline centered on provenance and time. The pipeline reconstructs scattered documents into partial traces and clearly exposes gaps that cannot be recovered. Third, through a leakage ladder, zero-parameter baselines, county-grouped evaluation, censoring audits, and failure gates, it shows that model performance cannot replace target validity. Fourth, it divides the evidence needed for reasonable-effort review into a “trace of effort evidence” and a “context of difficulty,” while showing that current data cannot directly train the two into an adequacy label. Fifth, it proposes a hybrid architecture organized by authority: the LMM proposes candidates and reconciles evidence, Model A ranks cases, Model B selects references, rules express explicit conditions, and humans accept facts and remain responsible for final actions. The new system must also write every human decision back as a versioned decision trace by design.

### 2.2 Five Research Questions

- **RQ1 (trace recoverability):** Which provenance-bearing decision-trace fragments can be recovered through multimodal reading, provenance tracking, deterministic safeguards, and internal multi-agent review, and which elements remain unidentifiable?
- **RQ2 (honest generalization):** Under county-grouped generalization, what internal associations appear for the mixed nongeographic evidence/covariate profile and for the county/formation geography prior? This comparison does not make a causal decomposition of their contributions.
- **RQ3 (target validity):** Which missing effort/action and deliberation traces prevent historical shortfall/difficulty from being interpreted as effort adequacy, and how do selective labels and censoring change the conclusions?
- **RQ4 (construct survival):** Which request-checklist and DN signals remain after rule freezing, internally blinded AI-agent reading, provenance adjudication, and bidirectional audits?
- **RQ5 (authority validity):** How should the failures of G9, Path B, and retrospective routing limit automation authority and the agent architecture?

### 2.3 Five-Part Validity Framework

We express a model claim as five questions that must all be answered:

1. **Measurement validity:** Was the field read from the correct page and visual state?
2. **Temporal validity:** Did the field already exist at the index time of the proposed decision?
3. **Construct validity:** Does the label actually measure the concept that must be judged?
4. **Generalization validity:** Does the evaluation block the real county, operator, template, and time structures?
5. **Authority validity:** Is the evidence from the first four questions sufficient to authorize the specific consequence?

These are conjunctive conditions, not scores that can compensate for one another. An outcome-state field read accurately from a page can still be temporally invalid. A shortfall model with a high AUC can still be invalid at the construct level. An internally reliable DN ranking still cannot automatically gain the authority to refuse a request.

### 2.4 Decision-Trace Framework and Evidence Decomposition for Reasonable-Effort Review

For case (i) at index time (t), an ideal decision trace can be written as:

\[
T_i(t)=\{S_i(t),E_i(t),D_i(t),L_i(t),P_t,R_i(t),A_i(t),U_i(t),\Pi_i(t)\},
\]

where (S) is the state visible at the time, (E) is source-linked effort/action evidence, (D) is the well and observed difficulty context, (L) is the deliberation through request, feedback, and revision, (P_t) is the applicable rule or policy version, (R) is the request or claim, (A) is the authorized action and its reason, (U) is uncertainty/override, and (\Pi) is the provenance relationship. The later outcome (O_i) is linked separately and does not backfill (T_i(t)).

The historical structured table observes only an incomplete projection (X_i) of this object and mixes in the later outcome (O_i). Training (P(O_i\mid X_i)) is therefore generally not the same as estimating the request-time reasonable-effort judgment:

\[
A_i^*=H\{E_i(t),D_i(t),L_i(t),P_t,\text{accepted evidence},\text{qualified expert standard}\}.
\]

To organize review without pretending to measure (A_i^*) automatically, we retain two descriptive evidence channels:

1. **Documented effort/evidence channel:** dated attempts, methods, tools, reached depths, obstructions, remedies, results, and sources;
2. **Difficulty-context channel:** request-time TD, well age, casing, coal, freshwater, observed obstructions, and reached depth.

The county/formation geography prior is kept separately as group background. It does not contain nongeographic well-condition covariates such as TD, well age, casing, water, or coal. Both evidence channels still require a deliberation/rule gate and a qualified reviewer before they can support a reasonable-effort judgment. This is a functional decomposition, not a causal factorization into two latent factors.

### 2.5 Evaluation Design and Statistical Reporting

Different experiments used different cross-validation designs and different forms of dispersion reporting. This paper does not interpret every “±” as the same kind of uncertainty.

- The main historical-shortfall analysis of 631 cases used county-grouped `StratifiedGroupKFold`: 5 folds × 5 split seeds. Each seed produced out-of-fold (OOF) predictions covering the full cohort, and the mean and SD were reported across five repeat-level metrics. For example, the 0.0090 in 0.7400±0.0090 is the dispersion of five split-level AUC values, not a confidence interval.
- Model A used `StratifiedGroupKFold(county)`, with 5 seeds × 5 folds. The training script summarized the AUCs of valid held-out folds. The 0.1097 in 0.8366±0.1097 is fold-AUC dispersion, not a repeat-level SD or a confidence interval.
- The Model B script shuffled rows for five seeds and then called `GroupKFold(shuffle=False)`. The actual held-out county sets did not change. We therefore report 0.418±0.047 as a Spearman fold summary for one effective five-fold county-grouped partition, not as independent 5×5 repeated cross-validation.
- The repeat-0 evidence-versus-geography comparison used the same OOF vector and reported a case bootstrap and a county-cluster bootstrap. It does not estimate the same quantity as the difference between five-repeat mean AUCs.
- G9 used a two-sided Fisher exact test and, under the frozen specification, reported refused-side cluster-collapse and tuning-untainted sensitivity tests. A probability-ordered p value from a discrete test and an equal-tail interval are not guaranteed to be exact duals.
- Small checklist samples used naive Clopper–Pearson intervals to show denominator uncertainty. Repeated readers were nested within cases, and model errors were correlated, so the observations were not treated as independent Bernoulli trials.
- Path B used paired AUC differences from the same 25 grouped folds and was judged by prefrozen absolute and incremental thresholds.

All classification results give priority to ROC AUC. Some shortfall results also report average precision, Brier score, and top-of-queue precision. The regression proxy uses Spearman correlation and bin-agreement rates. Except where a bootstrap or exact interval is explicitly listed, SD should not be read as a population confidence interval. The exploration of multiple models also did not produce a multiplicity-adjusted confirmatory analysis covering every comparison.

### 2.6 Terminology and Internal Identifiers

- **LMM:** large multimodal model, used for candidate evidence extraction or visual reconciliation.
- **HGB:** histogram gradient boosting; Model A is a classifier and Model B is a regressor.
- **OOF:** out-of-fold; evaluation based only on held-out predictions from records that were not used in the corresponding fit.
- **v6:** the sixth major revision of the historical multimodal extraction/modeling pipeline, not an external standard version.
- **G8:** the internal blind-reading reliability gate used to check track separation, vocabularies, and the stability of field emission.
- **G9:** the frozen association gate for DN in audited refusals versus historical approved-status cases; the primary comparison and both sensitivity conditions all had to pass before interpretation could be allowed.
- **Path B:** the frozen ceiling-break backtest that added checklist features to the old `y_needed_short` task.
- **D1:** shortfall normalized relative to TD. Historical references and current requests use different depth sources and must be labeled separately.
- **CK1–CK4:** four draft items in the current runtime checklist. They check the request form, effort narrative/evidence, decision-relevant depth numbers, and the timeliness of depth numbers, respectively.
- **Quadrant:** a 2×2 preliminary triage label defined by checklist pass/fail × tail yes/no; it is not an approval decision.
- **Decision trace:** a replayable decision unit organized by case, time, source, action, rule, reason, and authority. This study reconstructs fragments from historical archives, not complete deliberation.

## 3. Part I: Multimodal, Multi-Agent Reconstruction of Partial Decision Traces

### 3.1 Why Multimodality Was Necessary

Historical case files contain scanned text, handwritten numbers, checkboxes, stamps, well diagrams, tables that continue across pages, and application materials with inconsistent formats. Text-only OCR can help with retrieval, but it may lose visual meaning, such as whether a box was checked, which column contains a number, or which form a stamp belongs to. This study therefore treated OCR as a candidate-retrieval tool rather than the final judge of fact. OCR can tell the system, “the information may be here”; a rendered page can reject an OCR-only interpretation; and only a reviewer has the authority to accept a fact.

The v6 pipeline sent PDFs directly to a multimodal interface and designed visual fields to include the source file, page number, original text, evidence type, and time/depth track. A later full-corpus evidence review processed each of the 635 source-file directories. Seventeen oversized PDFs from 14 cases were first rendered at 150 dpi to avoid transfer limits. The model identifier in the configuration used a floating alias, and the batch requests did not fully fix every sampling parameter. This paper therefore does not describe extraction as deterministic in the mathematical sense. Reproducibility comes from archiving raw responses, derived tables, and code versions, not from assuming that a future rerun will produce exactly the same wording (Fig. 1).

### 3.2 Provenance Was Not Additional Metadata but Part of the Output

A structured value is complete only when it can answer the question, “Where was this read?” Visual evidence used the following provenance tuple:

\[
E=(\text{case},\text{source file},\text{page},\text{quote},\text{visual state},\text{track},\text{timing}).
\]

Well attributes and some historical labels came from a legacy Excel register. The most accurate statement is therefore that each field should be traceable to an original PDF page, the legacy register, or a deterministic derived ledger. It would be too broad to claim that “every field came from a PDF page.” Repairs to evidence pointers during later audits showed that a correct field value does not guarantee a correct provenance path. Citation accuracy must be evaluated as a separate endpoint.

This provenance tuple is an **evidence object**, not a complete decision trace. A complete trace also needs the actor, actions and alternatives, back-and-forth revisions, the applicable rule version, the final reason, and any override. Some elements were never recorded in the historical PDFs or could not be linked reliably. The pipeline can therefore recover only visible fragments. It cannot use an LMM-generated narrative to fill in the real deliberation.

### 3.3 Strict Separation of Three Depth and Time Tracks

To replay the state at the index time, the pipeline kept at least three kinds of state separate:

- **Requested:** the stopping depth and reason proposed or stated by the operator at the time of application;
- **Approved:** the depth later approved or limited by DEP;
- **Certificate/final state:** the final state observed after completion, the plugging certificate, or other outcome-state information.

The TD anchor, observed obstruction, maximum reached depth, and proposed stop also retained separate provenance and time attributes. Backfilling certificate depth as a request-time input creates temporal leakage. Using an approved attainable bottom as a predictive feature can create an outcome-state loop. Separating time tracks is not cosmetic database work. It is necessary to prevent a later outcome from overwriting decision state and to make the partial trace replayable.

### 3.4 Deterministic Safeguards, Quarantine, and Negative Authority

Three kinds of safeguards were applied after multimodal output: numerical plausibility, evidence/current-job context, and time-track consistency. Suspicious results were not quietly corrected or absorbed as missing values. They entered quarantine for review. The final v6 artifacts contained 10,856 depth-evidence rows and 436 quarantine records and recorded 21 current-job safeguard events.

This design gives different components different forms of “negative authority.” OCR can propose a candidate but cannot establish a fact. A visual page can overturn an OCR-only candidate. A safeguard can prevent a suspicious field from entering downstream use. A researcher or reviewer can accept, correct, or reject a field. The value of having multiple components is not voting; it is the ability to locate an error along the provenance chain.

### 3.5 Multi-Agent Blind Reading and Researcher Adjudication

Because human domain-expert resources were limited, the project used independently run AI-agent blind reading, a frozen emission vocabulary, source-page review, and researcher/user adjudication. The goal was to find unclear definitions, pointer errors, and reader disagreement, not to replace expert truth with the claim that “several models agree.”

The main G8 run included 23 cases × 2 reads = 46 reader outputs. The reliability subset included 18 cases × 2 reads = 36 outputs, while 5 anchor cases × 2 reads = 10 outputs were excluded from the reliability denominator. The confirmation panel included 6 cases × 2 reads = 12 outputs. Counts such as 11/18, 14/18, 6/6, and 4/6 for different fields were agreement counts, not accuracy rates. The combined claim of “0/58 contamination” across the main and confirmation runs was also based on nested and correlated outputs, so confidence cannot be calculated as if they were 58 independent human judgments.

This internal mechanism successfully led to narrower rules, retired fields, a reversal in DN coding, and repaired provenance. However, it may still contain correlated model errors. This paper therefore consistently calls it “internally independent AI-agent blind reading with researcher adjudication,” not “external validation by independent human experts.”

### 3.6 When Human Experts Enter the Loop

This study distinguishes two forms of human-in-the-loop review:

1. **Internal human oversight that has already occurred:** researchers verified source pages, adjudicated some disagreements, reviewed DN-positive cases, and sampled negative cases;
2. **Independent expert validation that remains to be completed:** two domain experts will independently code a new request-time cohort, a third expert will adjudicate, and the study will measure category accuracy, depth error, citation accuracy, track contamination, and temporal contamination.

A future system should require escalation to a qualified reviewer when a source page cannot be located; OCR conflicts with the visual state; requested, approved, and final-state tracks conflict; a rule has major consequences; Model A, Model B, checklist, or compliance signals disagree; or a case falls outside the training or reference distribution. The expert does not sign after an automated decision. The expert verifies a field before it becomes eligible to produce consequences.

### 3.7 What the Pipeline Produced and What It Did Not Prove

The final v6 merge produced 633 unique case-level rows and 10,856 depth rows. Two large batches had HTTP success counts of 818/818 and 483/483. These numbers show transport completeness, not case accuracy. In a fixed, targeted 40-case regression set, three predefined fields—observed-depth presence, physical-blocker presence, and historical-seal presence—each achieved precision and recall of 1.0. Because the set was designed around known failure modes rather than drawn as a random sample of the full population, the result cannot be generalized to the full corpus, low-quality scans, or every field.

The answer to RQ1 is therefore a limited yes: the pipeline produced traceable, quarantinable, and auditable evidence artifacts. It has not established an independent expert reference standard, nor has it shown that the system saves time or reduces errors in real review.

### 3.8 Three “Continents” of Decision-Trace Completeness

The semantic composition and trace coverage of the 170-column schema were highly structured rather than uniformly missing at random (Figs. 2–3):

- Administrative and well attributes were nearly complete, including operator request at 96.0%, alternative-method filed at 94.8%, and coal-seam indicator at 90.8%;
- Application narratives had moderate coverage, including request evidence at 41.0%, causal reason at 36.8%, and any struggle at 31.8%;
- Most depth evidence had approximately 15%–30% coverage, including predecision observed at 18.9%, physical blocker at 14.6%, and historical seal at 29.5%;
- Almost all binary flags for detailed work-record difficulty, remedy, and obstruction were below 2.5%.

This distinction is important. Numerical event counts were not missing. The 424 cases contained 2,874 dated events; 423/424 cases had at least one, with a median of 6 and a maximum of 30. However, 2,375/2,874 (82.6%) were placed only in the coarse `other` phase, compared with 457 plugging events and 42 cleanout events. In other words, the archive did not “lack work records.” It lacked stable, detailed, and cross-document standardized labels for difficulties and methods.

### 3.9 Why Low Documentation Rates Cannot Be Used to Infer Effort/Action Traces

The same types of difficulty had sharply different documentation rates in application narratives and in separate work-record aggregations: cave-in/collapse was 17.45% versus 0.24% (about 74 times higher); hard fill/no progress was 12.5% versus 2.36% (about 5.3 times); stuck/lost tools was 5.90% versus 1.89% (about 3.1 times); casing failure was 3.77% versus 1.18% (about 3.2 times); and freshwater-at-depth was 0.94% versus 0.24% (about 4 times). Mine-through was 7.31% versus zero, so a finite ratio cannot be given.

These are differences in traces that were “documented or recognized by the current extraction rules,” not differences in actual event rates. The pattern is consistent with selective narrative at request time, but it does not prove that operators encountered difficulties only when making a request (Fig. 4).

Tool documentation was even sparser. Only 197/2,874 events contained any tool token, meaning that 93.15% had no token. The deployment method was unknown for 2,687/2,874 events (93.49%). The 169 raw tokens included synonyms, mixed levels of detail, and OCR noise; they cannot be interpreted as 169 separate tools. Remediation flags in the work-record layer were all no higher than 0.71%, and even the highest rate in the application-narrative layer was only about 1.65%. “No tool recorded” therefore cannot be interpreted as “no tool used,” and the current archive is not sufficient for reliable training of a tool/remedy model (Fig. 5).

These results are direct empirical evidence of the decision-trace deficit. The system can collect and display difficulties that appear in the documents, but it cannot interpret document silence as an absence of effort. It also cannot judge the adequacy of effort from sparse tool fields. An integrated view of effort-related coverage further shows that, although 99.8% of cases had a ledger row, only 6.1% had explicit attempted-and-failed evidence (Fig. 6). Having an archive is not the same as having a trainable, replayable action trace.

## 4. Part II: How to Choose the Right Model—Choose the Right Question First

### 4.1 The Early Step-Location Task: How a Circular Label Produced a High Score

The project first tried to locate, within the event sequence of each approved case, the step that had historically been selected as the final position. The final authoritative audit set contained 103 cases and 379 event rows and used case-grouped cross-validation. On the surface, gradient boosting using depth relative to TD achieved an AUC of 0.916. When depth relative to the approved attainable bottom was used, GB and RF reached 0.954 and 0.958.

However, a rule with no learned parameters—selecting the deepest event in each case—already matched the label in 79/103 cases (76.7%). Effort-only GB had an AUC of about 0.717 and a top-1 hit rate of 64.1%, while the feature based on approved attainable bottom directly contained the outcome-state decision. The correct conclusion was not that “the model improved from 0.72 to 0.96.” Instead:

- 0.717 represented limited process signals;
- 0.916 mainly reproduced the definitional structure that “the label is usually the deepest event”;
- 0.954–0.958 used the outcome-state approval anchor and therefore involved leakage.

This within-case track was therefore retired and was no longer interpreted as a prospective approval model. This was the project’s first correction in problem definition and the starting point for target auditing. Before any complex model is used, a zero-parameter baseline should test whether ranking, a maximum value, or an outcome-state field already defines the label.

### 4.2 RQ2: Under Grouped Generalization, Different “High Scores” Came from Different Tasks

The project then moved to a case-level historical shortfall proxy based on 631 cases. The main evaluation used five-fold `StratifiedGroupKFold` with county as the group and repeated it with five split seeds. This design more closely represents use with new geographic groups than random stratification does.

|---|---|---|---:|---|
| County-grouped | Nongeographic request evidence + well-context covariates | HGB | 0.7400 (0.0090) | Internal shortfall ranking from a mixed profile; not an effort model |
| County-grouped | v6 predecision depth | HGB | 0.7383 (0.0080) | Richer depth evidence did not improve AUC |
| County-grouped | Legacy 11 | Logistic L2 | 0.7353 (0.0039) | A simple baseline remained close to the best single model |
| County-grouped | Geography only | HGB | 0.6247 (0.0061) | County/formation prior alone had limited performance |
| Random stratified | v6 predecision depth | HGB | 0.7858 (0.0071) | Optimistic comparison after ignoring groups |

In the retained repeat-0 shared-OOF vector, the nongeographic evidence/covariates profile had an AUC of 0.7454, compared with 0.6207 for the standalone geography prior. The difference was 0.1247, with a county-cluster bootstrap interval of 0.0578–0.2244. Three kinds of concepts must be separated here. The geography-only profile was county/formation. TD, well age, casing, water, and coal were nongeographic well/environmental context. Request narratives, events, and depth evidence were document evidence. The current `evidence/covariates` profile mixed the last two categories. It was therefore not an effort-only profile, and the 0.1247 difference cannot estimate “how much more effort matters than geography.”

The often-cited gain of about +0.056 for HGB over a linear model came from a different shared-OOF, random-stratified paired bootstrap, not from the repeated county-grouped main analysis above. It should be treated as secondary evidence. The equal-weight three-model ensemble had a five-grouped-split summary AUC of 0.759±0.005. The strongest directly reproducible retained repeat-0 artifact had AUC 0.7567, AP 0.6943, and Brier 0.1921. It shows that several related predictors can improve historical shortfall ranking, but they do not change the label construct.

The key to RQ2 is not to assign causal percentages to evidence, geography, model class, and feature richness. It is to distinguish four empirical findings: evidence/covariates contained more ranking information than geography alone; geographic grouping substantially reduced optimistic estimates; nonlinearity added value in some comparisons; and adding richer v6 features did not break through an internal ceiling of about 0.74–0.76. The performance ceiling was determined by the task and label, not only by feature engineering.

### 4.3 RQ3: Difficulty Was Not Adequacy

Historical shortfall can result from difficult geology, an allowed attainable-bottom adjustment, the completion process, documentation rules, or genuine inadequacy. Labels are especially selective in approved-only or outcome-state archives. Successfully completed difficult cases accumulate more later evidence, while cases that were refused or returned for more information may remain in a different state.

The project conducted an exploratory censoring audit of 89 cases in which certificate depth was approximately equal to the approved attainable bottom. The retained ensemble had an AUC of 0.7567 under the original labels, 0.7423 after these possibly approved-and-completed cases were excluded, and 0.6885 after they were relabeled as negative. This sensitivity analysis cannot estimate new deployment performance, but it is enough to reject the stronger interpretation: the same historical shortfall label could not consistently distinguish “difficult but completed under the approved plan” from “inadequate effort.”

The model with an AUC of about 0.76 was therefore renamed a shortfall-risk/difficulty ranking rather than an adequacy classifier. Reasonable-effort review can display documented effort/action evidence and difficulty context separately, but it cannot add them together into a historical adequacy score. It also needs the rule/version in effect at the time, back-and-forth deliberation, accepted source fields, and the normative judgment of a qualified reviewer. The research task was therefore narrowed: first identify documented non-routine difficulty, and then ask whether the current proposed shortfall needs additional explanation when compared with a coarse group of similar historical settings.

### 4.4 RQ4: Construct Narrowing for the Checklist and DN

The request-package checklist separated factual extraction from normative judgment and continued to keep requested, approved, and certificate tracks separate. The research instrument’s development from v1 to v1.4 was not intended to improve agreement rates for appearance. In response to failures on frozen panels, the project removed unstable tokens, narrowed definitions, and repaired provenance pointers. The current runtime CK1–CK4 is labeled **v1.5-DRAFT**. It adds engineering rules such as depth-number presence and timeliness, has not completed legal or independent-expert validation, and should not be described as the same version as the completed v1.4 internal blind reading.

**Table 3. Checklist/DN signals that survived, were revised, or were retired in frozen internal blind reading**

| Signal/rule | Main panel | Confirmation/audit | Decision | Permitted interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| Request A/B track separation | 17/18 agreement | Not separately re-estimated | Retained | Internally stable track definition |
| S2 strict vocabulary | 11/18 | 6/6 after clarification | Retained after revision | Vocabulary must be narrowed; the original 11/18 cannot support a claim of high reliability |
| S4 exact token set | 14/18 | 4/6 | Unstable token retired | Exact token is unsuitable for a downstream rule |
| S4 parent binary | 18/18 | 6/6 | Binary parent retained | Only the coarser binary construct is retained |
| C3 | One adjudicated false positive during development | 6/6 agreement; 0 adjudicated false positives | Cautiously retained | Small-sample internal stability, not population specificity |
| Cross-track contamination | 0/46 outputs | 0/58 outputs in total | Track-separation rule retained | Outputs were nested and correlated, not 58 independent trials |
| DN positive construct | 112/424 positive; each positive internally adjudicated | 5 positive→negative, 1 negative→positive, 15 pointer repairs | Retained as documented difficulty | Does not generalize to effort/adequacy |
| DN negative side | Not exhaustive | 0 flips in a 20-case blind sample | Supported only by the sample | Cannot claim that all negative cases were validated |

Agreement among the internal readers supports the greater stability of the selected operational definitions within this system. It does not establish a legal standard or prove population accuracy.

DN was defined as **documented non-routine difficulty**: the materials contained locatable evidence of a non-routine difficulty. It did not mean actual effort, diligence, compliance, adequacy, approval, or refusal. Among 424 cases, internal case-by-case project adjudication identified 112 DN-positive cases, including 108 major and 4 minor cases. DN was present in 105 of 411 historical approved-status cases (25.5%) and 7 of 13 audited refusals (53.8%). The positive side received internal case-by-case adjudication, while the negative side had only a blinded sample audit of 20 cases. The 424 labels therefore cannot be described as an independent expert gold standard.

The audit trail listed five substantive positive→negative reversals, one negative→positive reversal, and 15 evidence-pointer repairs. Another narrative source stated “six positive→negative” but listed only five cases, and its taxonomy also contained five. This paper uses the enumerable value of five and discloses the inconsistency between sources.

### 4.5 From 108 Archival Variables to 17 Trace-Compatible Fields

The archival DN research model used 108 features, including post-work variables such as the presence of certificate/completion OCR. It could therefore be used only for archival research. In full-refit permutation importance, only seven features were reported as positive: deepest casing depth, 0.00141; alternative-method narrative event count, 0.00046; TD, 0.00008; causal reason, 0.00003; any narrative struggle, 0.00002; maximum observed/TD, 0.00001; and well age, 0.00001. The remaining 101 features were approximately zero in that calculation.

These very small values can be used only for relative ranking. They are not causal effects and do not mean that the 101 columns were literally zero. The 108-feature archival HGB had an internal county-grouped AUC point estimate of 0.8412±0.1136. The 17-field request-time-compatible Model A had 0.8366±0.1097, a difference of about 0.0046. Because no formal equivalence test was performed, we say only that the point estimates were close, not that they were statistically equivalent (Fig. 7).

Model A and Model B share the following 17 fields:

1. TD;
2. Well age;
3. Casing count;
4. Deepest casing depth;
5. Coal-seam indicator;
6. Freshwater indicator;
7. File count;
8. Total current-job event count;
9. Cleanout event count;
10. Effort/work event count;
11. Whether an alternative-method form was submitted;
12. Whether an attempted-and-failed narrative was present;
13. Whether a causal reason was present;
14. Maximum reached depth;
15. Obstruction depth/indicator;
16. Maximum reached/TD;
17. Whether a proposed stop was entered.

The models do not read the numeric proposed-stop value. That value is used only in the later D1 calculation. County, operator, and permit identity are not among these 17 serving fields, so Models A and B cannot be called geography models. The first six fields are well/environmental context, not a geographic profile. The file, event, and narrative fields are only a very small subset of the documented-effort trace, not a complete effort history. Certificate/completion fields were excluded. “Request-time/trace-compatible” means only that a field can be collected directly at the time of application. It does not mean that the 17 fields are sufficient to represent a complete decision trace or that they have been validated on a real prospective cohort.

### 4.6 Model A: Documented-Difficulty Trace Triage—Deciding “Who Is Seen First”

Model A was trained on the project-defined DN label. It outputs a `dn_score` that orders the human review queue from high to low. Its official internal metric came from `StratifiedGroupKFold(county)` with 5 seeds × 5 folds and was 0.8366±0.1097. Here, the SD is the dispersion of AUC across valid held-out folds. It is not “83.66% accuracy,” a repeat-level SD, or a confidence interval, and it does not show that the score is a calibrated probability.

Model A allocates scarce attention. When the materials look more like historical DN-positive cases, a reviewer can examine their sources and difficulty narratives earlier. Model A does not enter the 2×2 quadrant formula, assign a difficulty bin, determine whether effort was adequate, or predict approval probability. Queue ranking can still affect waiting time and the order of attention, so it is not a “zero-consequence” number. Formal use would still need to measure queue burdens across groups such as operators, counties, and document-quality levels.

### 4.7 Model B: Empirical Context/Reference Selector—Deciding “Which Ruler to Use”

Model B did not target objective engineering difficulty labeled by human experts. Its target was the teacher score from the frozen historical shortfall ensemble. Using 423 retrospective cases with available teacher scores, it learned a mapping from 17 fields to the teacher score and then used frozen cutpoints to select a Q1–Q5 reference bin. It did not use the county/formation geography-only profile and did not recover true causal geologic difficulty. `Difficulty proxy` was only an engineering name for coarse historical reference selection.

Although the training script looped over five seeds, each loop only shuffled the rows before calling `GroupKFold(shuffle=False)`. The held-out county sets were actually the same. The result of 0.418±0.047 should therefore be described precisely as the **fold-mean Spearman correlation and fold SD from one five-fold county-grouped cross-validation partition**, not as 5×5 independent repeated cross-validation or a confidence interval. Model B had exact-bin agreement of 35.5% with the teacher and agreement within ±1 bin of 72.3%. In other words, 27.7% of cases differed by at least two bins (Fig. 8).

At runtime, Model B has the following indirect role:

\[
\widehat{s}_{B}(x)\rightarrow \widehat{Q}\in\{1,2,3,4,5\}
\rightarrow P_{90,\widehat{Q}}
\rightarrow \text{tail flag}.
\]

For a current application, the system separately calculates:

\[
D1_{request}=\operatorname{clip}\left(\frac{TD-proposed\ stop}{TD},0,1\right).
\]

If `D1_request` is above the historical P90 of the reference bin selected by Model B, an escalation flag is generated. This flag is then combined with the checklist to form the preliminary quadrant. The most accurate summary is therefore: **A decides who is seen first, and B decides which historical ruler to use. B does not make the final decision, but choosing the wrong ruler can change the tail flag and the initial route.**

### 4.8 Evidence for “Group First, Then Compare” and the Unresolved Transport Assumption

Among 354 historical reference records that were operationally defined in code as non-audited refusals, the locked teacher score divided cases into bins of 70–71 records each. Reference eligibility required a scoreable record, `chk_refused_flag=0`, and nonsuspect TD. It did not require every record to have a newly reconfirmed approved status. The main text therefore cannot call them “354 confirmed approved precedents.”

Across teacher-defined Q1→Q5, median historical outcome-label shortfall rose from 0.037 to 0.281, an approximately 7.6-fold difference between endpoints. DN prevalence rose from 8.5% to 40.8%, an approximately 4.8-fold difference. This shows a clear association between teacher strata, historical shortfall, and DN, but it does not create an independent external truth for difficulty (Fig. 9).

More importantly, historical-reference D1 was calculated from an outcome-state label:

\[
D1_{ref}=\frac{TD-label\_bottom\_of\_plug}{TD},
\]

while a new application uses the proposed stop. Placing `D1_request` and `D1_ref` on the same empirical scale is a transport assumption that has not been prospectively validated, not a proven equivalence.

The teacher-stratum P90 values were 0.332, 0.276, 0.415, 0.456, and 0.552. The fact that the P90 for Q1 was higher than the one for Q2 also signals finite-sample noise. For example, a `D1_request=0.45` would fall at approximate percentiles of 95.6%, 99.5%, 91.7%, 89.5%, and 73.8% across the five bins. It would therefore trigger a tail flag in Q1–Q3 but not in Q4–Q5. This example shows that conditioned comparison can change how review attention is allocated for the same shortfall. It does not prove that an application in a more difficult case is automatically reasonable (Fig. 10).

### 4.9 Pooled Threshold Versus Conditioned Threshold: Counterfactual Review Burden, Not Proof of Fairness

If all reference records shared a pooled P90 of 0.455, the proportions flagged in teacher Q1–Q5 would be 4.2%, 1.4%, 8.6%, 11.3%, and 25.4%. Q5 would be flagged about 18 times as often as Q2. If P90 were defined within each teacher bin, about 9.9%–10.0% would be flagged in every bin. This result shows that a pooled threshold would concentrate nominal review volume in the historical difficulty strata, while stratified percentiles can make nominal tail mass more even within the teacher reference (Fig. 11).

However, “about 10% in each bin” is largely guaranteed by the definition of P90. More importantly, Figs. 2–4 used the real locked teacher score to assign bins, while Model B’s proxy assigns bins at runtime. These figures are therefore a **teacher-conditioned proof of concept**, not end-to-end Model B calibration. They cannot support claims that “the deployed system has already achieved a 10% rate in each bin,” “fairness has been proven,” or “bias has been eliminated.” What must actually be tested is whether proxy-selected bins produce acceptable tail rates, false escalations, operator burden, and reviewer outcomes in new request-time data.

### 4.10 RQ5: How Three Failures Closed Off Authority

The project did not place negative results at the end of the paper as disclaimers. It specified system consequences for failures in advance.

**Table 4. Failure-gate results and their authority consequences**

| Gate | Result | Prespecified interpretation | Authority consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| G9 primary comparison | DN: 7/13 refused vs. 105/411 approved-status; sample OR 3.40; equal-tail exact 95% CI 0.951–12.500; Fisher p=0.0477 | Nominal primary signal with a very small sample | Insufficient by itself to authorize action |
| G9 duplicate-cluster sensitivity | 5/10, p=0.136 | Required supportive p<0.10 | Failed |
| G9 tuning-untainted sensitivity | 4/10, OR 1.94, p=0.291 | Required supportive p<0.10 | Failed; G9 BLOCKED |
| Path B | Combined AUC 0.6841; Δ0.0107 relative to auto (SD 0.0431); positive in 11 of 25 folds | Combined mean had to be >0.764 and meet the increment condition | Did not break through the ceiling |
| Anomaly | Internal OOF tail 10.17% at a nominal 10% | Checked only implementation/nominal tail | Did not gain an insufficiency meaning |
| Routing smoke test | 5 of 7 depth-related refusals entered `FAST_LANE` | Routing direction should agree with known refusal mechanisms | Final-state retrospective routing was rejected |

In G9, 3.40 was the cross-product sample odds ratio. The fixed-margin noncentral-hypergeometric equal-tail interval included 1, while the probability-ordered two-sided Fisher p value was slightly below 0.05. These are not exact duals in a discrete test. Because both prefrozen sensitivity conditions failed, the primary p value cannot be selected by itself and repackaged as success.

Path B tested whether adding the existing checklist to the old historical shortfall task could break through the frozen ceiling. It did not prove that any future request-time human label would have no value. The routing smoke-test failure was more direct: approved/completed outcome-state information made refused cases look like successful cases. The research team did not retune thresholds after seeing the result. It disabled retrospective approval/refusal routing.

This creates **failure-gated authority**:

- Early circularity closed off a decision interpretation for step location;
- The censoring audit closed off the shortfall→effort-adequacy interpretation;
- G9 failure closed off authority to turn DN into refusal/adequacy;
- Path-B failure closed off the claim that the checklist had solved the old label ceiling;
- Anomaly retained only an explanation-needed flag;
- Routing-smoke failure closed off automatic fast or adverse routing based on final state.

## 5. Part III: Agent Architecture—From Consuming Trace-Poor Archives to Producing Prospective Decision Traces

### 5.1 “Multi-Agent” Had Different Meanings in Research and at Runtime

The research stage did use several agent roles: case-by-case multimodal extraction, independent blind reading, provenance reconciliation, failure review, and researcher adjudication. The current runtime prototype, however, is not an autonomous system with a planner, delegation, and negotiation among multiple agents. It is a hybrid pipeline with two optional LMM services, two conventional ML models, deterministic rules, similar-text retrieval, and a human review interface.

This paper therefore uses “multi-agent evidence engineering” to describe the division of research and review tasks, and “hybrid authority architecture” to describe the runtime. The innovation is authority separation and trace production, not the number of agents.

### 5.2 The Actual Wiring of the Current Prototype

The current code flow can be summarized as:

```text
PDF → optional LMM prefill → operator/user confirmation
                                  ↓
structured validation → immediate preliminary scoring
    ├─ compliance rules
    ├─ CK1–CK4 checklist
    ├─ Model A → dn_prescreen → review queue order
    ├─ Model B → proxy bin → in-bin P90 tail
    └─ RAG context / supplement hint
                                  ↓
                    preliminary review package
                                  ↓
         post-submit LMM reconciliation (advisory)
                                  ↓
                    reviewer-selected action
```

`/prefill` renders up to 30 PDF pages and generates candidate values and `{file,page}` pointers for six panels. The front end marks them as suggested and waits for confirmation. A structured submission uses Pydantic to validate conditions including attestation, depth≤TD, dates, measurement methods, failed-attempt narratives, and future dates. Immediately after submission, the system calculates Models A and B, the checklist, compliance, and anomaly, and then saves an initial review package.

The files are then uploaded and queued for LMM reconciliation. This agent visually checks the presence, date, tense, and depth of the DN claim, but it is currently a Stage-1 advisory tool. It does not modify CK2, the original quadrant, or Model A’s queue order. The reviewer can later choose a separate action, and the Decision is stored separately from the machine quadrant. The current record does not yet consistently link all back-and-forth revisions, applicable policy versions, field-by-field acceptance, and mandatory rationale. It is therefore not a complete decision-trace ledger.

The current effort-path 2×2 formula is:

**Table 5. Preliminary 2×2 routing in the current prototype**

| v1.5-DRAFT checklist | In-bin tail selected by Model B | Current preliminary label | Meaning permitted in this paper |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pass | No | `FAST_LANE` | Lower nominal escalation priority; must not be equated directly with approval |
| Pass | Yes | `DEEP_REVIEW` | Materials are relatively complete, but shortfall is in the tail of the selected empirical reference bin |
| Fail | No | `SUPPLEMENT` | Additional checklist-specified materials are needed |
| Fail | Yes | `PUSHBACK` | Missing items and a tail position; may trigger only higher-level human review |

If the compliance rules return red, the current code overrides the label with `COMPLIANCE_REVIEW`. If the anomaly cannot be scored, only checklist status is used to produce `FAST_LANE` or `SUPPLEMENT`. These labels are current software strings. The wording for `FAST_LANE` still carries an approval-like meaning and cannot be treated in this paper as an authorized regulatory action.

### 5.3 Target Architecture: Accept Evidence Before Allowing Consequences

Based on the failure results, the target architecture should change the order to the following (Fig. 12):

```text
request-time structured intake
→ claim-to-source reconciliation
→ qualified reviewer accepts relevant fields
→ bounded ML / checklist / compliance / retrieval
→ non-decisional review routing
→ human decision + mandatory reasons
→ versioned decision-trace ledger
→ prospective labels derived only after validation
```

The central change is not to add a “human approval” button at the end. Human acceptance must move forward in the process. Any adverse route, expedited route, or route that may change the intensity of review can take effect only after a qualified reviewer opens the source page, checks the track, and actively accepts the relevant fields. Every request, supplemental submission, field revision, rule version, human acceptance, override, decision reason, and later outcome is written to the ledger as a separate event. The system thus becomes not only a consumer of historical data but also an infrastructure for collecting future decision traces.

### 5.4 Minimum Authority of Each Component

| Component | Permitted responsibility | Currently affects preliminary quadrant? | Prohibited interpretation |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Prefill LMM | Propose editable fields and candidate source pages | May affect it indirectly after user acceptance | Automatically establish a fact or approve |
| Reconciliation LMM | Return reconciliation flags such as verified/mismatch/not_found | No; currently advisory | Already has veto power or has passed external blind evaluation |
| Model A | DN prescreen; order the human queue | Does not enter the quadrant | Effort adequacy, difficulty, or approval probability |
| Model B | Approximate teacher score; select a peer bin | Enters indirectly through the tail flag | Objective difficulty, effort score, or approval probability |
| Historical anomaly table | Compare current-request D1 with the selected reference bin | Yes, as the tail dimension | Violation, misconduct, or a reason for refusal |
| CK1–CK4 | Display documentation completeness, timeliness, and numerical conflicts | Currently enters directly | A complete legal standard or an unverified automatic veto |
| Compliance rules | Produce a red flag from three declared fields | Currently overrides the quadrant | An automatic legal judgment with validated recall |
| RAG | Return similar text as context | Does not directly change the quadrant | Validated precedent, approval probability, or model reasoning |
| Human reviewer | Verify sources, interpret rules, and select the final action | Final actor | Delegate responsibility to the model |
| Decision-trace ledger | Save versioned state, source, rule, revision, acceptance, reason, override, and later outcome | Not fully implemented | Current logs are already clean gold or complete deliberation |

### 5.5 Gaps That Remain Between the Current Prototype and the Target Architecture

The code audit shows that the following capabilities should still be described as future requirements rather than implemented protections:

1. The current quadrant is calculated and displayed before provenance reconciliation and reviewer acceptance. It can only be called a preliminary triage label.
2. Reconciliation does not yet truly gate CK2 or the route, and the planned future switch does not form a complete execution path.
3. The current API schema does not fully preserve the `field_provenance` sent by the front end, so the provenance-acceptance loop remains incomplete.
4. `file_names` can contain declared values or placeholders and does not guarantee that a corresponding UploadedFile exists in the back end. File count and CK2 can therefore be affected by weakly supported provenance.
5. A compliance red flag currently overrides the quadrant but does not truly stop or hide outputs from Models A and B or anomaly.
6. Lane text still contains decision language such as `Recommend approval`, which conflicts with the goal that a lane should not imply approval.
7. A reviewer can select a different action, but the system does not require preservation of field-by-field accept/reject choices, bin overrides, mandatory reasons, evidence snapshots, model/rule versions, or the request↔supplement↔revision chain.
8. The decision log is structured prototype feedback, not a complete decision trace or a set of validated clean-gold labels.
9. The 20-query validation of RAG is incomplete, and metadata and filtering still have defects. The phrase “verified precedents” is too strong.
10. Some LMM/PDF runtime dependencies and real-PDF end-to-end validation have not been fully incorporated into deployment requirements.

Disclosing these gaps does not weaken the paper. It directly puts the paper’s method into practice: the fact that a system can run a piece of code does not mean that the code has received the corresponding institutional authority.

### 5.6 Expert-in-the-Loop Strategy for Uncertain Cases

In the future, scarce human resources can be focused on five kinds of cases:

- The source is absent, page numbers conflict, or the visual state cannot be confirmed;
- Model B is near a bin boundary or disagrees with the reviewer’s selected bin;
- The checklist fails but the document context is complex;
- A compliance red flag may produce an adverse consequence;
- Model A ranking, Model B tail, rules, and similar cases give conflicting signals.

For these cases, a qualified domain reviewer should complete field-level acceptance/rejection, any necessary bin override, the final action, and a mandatory reason. The system must also save the index-time state, source pages, rule/policy version, request-feedback-revision chain, model version, uncertainty, override, and later outcome before it can produce auditable prospective decision traces. Only after independent validation may specific trace fields be derived into future training labels.

## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 Decision Traces Are the Missing Layer of Agent-Ready Data

Previous work on decision provenance argues that the inputs to a decision, the nature of the action, data flows, and later effects should be exposed to support auditing and accountability. It focuses on the full decision pipeline, not only on algorithmic explanation. The framework of [Singh, Cobbe, and Norval](https://doi.org/10.1109/ACCESS.2018.2887201) becomes even more important in agent workflows because an agent both reads organizational state and may trigger real actions.

This case study provides a concrete stress test of trace completeness. Among 424 cases, 99.8% had a dated ledger row, but only 6.1% had explicit attempted-and-failed evidence. Only 197 of 2,874 events had a tool token. Administrative fields had approximately 90%–96% coverage, while most detailed work/action flags were below 2.5%. This does not mean that “the institution has no data.” It means that the data were not stored in a structure that could replay a reasonable-effort judgment. Multimodal technology can recover recorded fragments, but it cannot create unrecorded meeting judgments, telephone discussions, rule versions, or alternatives.

Agent-ready data therefore do not simply mean more rows, tokens, or embeddings. They require time-indexed, source-linked, actor-aware, rule-versioned, and reason-bearing traces. The system contribution of this paper is also not limited to consuming old archives. It designs an intake and review ledger that can produce this kind of trace in future workflows.

### 6.2 Different AUCs Were Not Continuous Improvements to the Same Model

The project produced numbers such as approximately 0.916, 0.759, 0.841, and 0.837. They belonged to different units, index times, targets, feature windows, and permitted actions. The 0.916 came from the early circular step-location task; approximately 0.759 came from the historical shortfall ensemble; 0.841 came from the archival DN research model that included post-work fields; and 0.837 came from the 17-field request-time-compatible DN prescreen. Presenting them as a sequence of model improvements from “0.72→0.92→0.84” would be a category error.

A model result must be reported together with the following five-part tuple:

\[
(\text{unit},\text{index time},\text{target},\text{evidence window},\text{permitted action}).
\]

Without this tuple, AUC is not a scientific conclusion. It is only a number separated from its problem definition.

### 6.3 Negative Results Were Architectural Inputs, Not an Appendix

The project’s most valuable results included the following: complex features did not break through the ceiling; adding cases did not improve the grouped AUC point estimate; refusal ground truth collapsed after source auditing; G9 sensitivity tests failed; Path B missed its threshold; and outcome-state routing pointed in the opposite direction. Every failure removed a form of authority rather than triggering a post-hoc change in threshold.

This method can be called failure-gated authority. It is stronger than “deploy first, then explain the risks in the limitations” because the consequence of failure occurs before a model output receives institutional meaning.

### 6.4 Provenance and Trace Correctness Should Be Model-Evaluation Endpoints

Accuracy in document AI has at least four dimensions: field value, citation, track, and time. A decision-trace system must also evaluate whether the actor, action, rule version, revision links, reason, and override are connected correctly. Even if a number is correct, it is still unsuitable as request-time evidence if it cites the wrong page or comes from a completion certificate. Future evaluation should separately report categorical accuracy; depth accuracy at exact/±25/±50 ft; citation correctness; requested/approved/final-state contamination; index-time contamination; trace-link completeness; and false linkage.

### 6.5 Difficulty Conditioning Was a Design for Review Volume, Not a Normative Judgment

Using different empirical P90 thresholds across teacher strata addresses an operational question: a shared threshold should not make a historically difficult stratum carry a disproportionate nominal review burden. It does not prove that historical approvals were reasonable or define reasonable effort under the law. In particular, the historical reference uses outcome-label shortfall, while a new application uses proposed shortfall. This transport must be validated in a prospective cohort.

Model B should also be understood as a reference selector, not an estimator of true difficulty. Exact-bin agreement was only 35.5%, so its output cannot support opaque automated consequences. A future interface could display continuous uncertainty, sensitivity to neighboring bins, and reviewer override together. If the P90 of a neighboring bin would change the flag, the system should escalate automatically rather than choose silently.

### 6.6 Hybrid Accountability Was Not Simply “Model Plus Rules”

Models are good at ranking and approximation; rules are good at expressing clear, reviewable conditions; LMMs are good at proposing candidates from mixed-format pages; and humans are good at interpreting context and accepting normative responsibility. The key to a hybrid architecture is not merely adding all four together. It is making sure that no layer can exceed its authority:

- The LMM has proposal/recall authority but no factual authority;
- Model A has attention-allocation authority but no adequacy authority;
- Model B has reference-selection authority but no objective-difficulty authority;
- Deterministic rules have visible-flag authority but no authority for complete legal adjudication;
- Human reviewers have evidence-acceptance and decision authority.

### 6.7 Transferability

This framework can transfer to document-heavy settings such as permit review, environmental remediation, occupational safety, benefit decisions, and infrastructure inspections. It can also explain situations in contract exceptions, customer handling, or discount approval where “the outcome value exists, but the policy version, reason for the exception, and approval chain are missing.” This comparison shows only the general usefulness of the decision-trace schema; it is not an empirical result of this project. The specific models, vocabularies, and checklists cannot transfer directly. Each setting must redefine its construct, index time, source types, real group structure, and allowed action.

## 7. Limitations

First, the historical materials did not fully preserve meetings, telephone calls, emails, policy versions, abandoned alternatives, or human reasons that were never written into the documents. This paper reconstructs provenance-bearing trace fragments, not a complete replay of actual deliberation. An LMM cannot infer a missing element and then write it as a fact.

Second, the final checklist, DN, and refusal mechanisms do not have an external gold standard from independent domain experts. Agreement among related AI agents may reflect shared system errors.

Third, the same universe of 635 case documents contributed to failure discovery, schema revision, threshold development, DN coding, model training, anomaly analysis, and prototype construction. Freezing within Git reduced some adaptive reuse, but this was not a completely untouched external test.

Fourth, county-grouped cross-validation did not block operator, related wells, repeated templates, form revisions, or time. Model B’s five seeds also did not create independent repeated folds; its ±0.047 was only the fold SD across five folds.

Fifth, the 277 of 912 directories without local source PDFs may be systematically related to age, operator, county, complexity, or outcome. Digital availability itself may have selected the sample.

Sixth, there were only 13 refusal cases, and their mechanisms were heterogeneous. Any nominal p value and post-hoc mechanism comparison are highly fragile. A fold SD is also not a population confidence interval.

Seventh, the 354 historical-reference cases were an operationally defined non-refused set, not a set of approved precedents that had all been reverified. Their D1 used the outcome-state field `label_bottom_of_plug`, and its comparability with `proposed_stop` in a new application has not been validated.

Eighth, both Model A’s DN labels and Model B’s teacher came from the same retrospective archive. A request-time-compatible feature definition does not equal request-time external validation. The functional division between Models A and B is also not causal identification of effort and difficulty.

Ninth, the historical three-category phase and the production ten-category `EVENT_TYPES` do not have an expert crosswalk. Historical `n_effort_work_events` and intake `n_effort_events` do not have fully matching meanings (Fig. 13).

Tenth, the model alias, some runtime parameters, and dependencies were not fully fixed. Archived responses improve auditability but cannot guarantee word-for-word reproduction in the future.

Eleventh, there is currently no prospective reviewer study. No evidence shows that the system shortens review time, improves consistency, produces better environmental outcomes, or prevents automation bias.

## 8. Prospective Validation Plan

The next stage should validate the data and the human-system interface before considering model retraining.

### 8.1 A New Request-Time Decision-Trace Cohort

Only forms, attachments, and fields that already exist when an application is submitted should be retained. The index time should be frozen, and certificate/final-state backfilling should be prohibited. Each request, agency feedback, supplement, revision, field observation, field acceptance, rule/policy version, override, decision reason, and later outcome should be saved as a separate event with an actor, timestamp, and source link. External holdouts should be created by operator, time, template family, and county, and rules for eligibility, missing data, thresholds, and model selection should be registered in advance. In addition to model metrics, evaluation should report trace-field capture rate, source-link correctness, revision-chain completeness, false linkage, and reason completeness.

### 8.2 Independent Expert Reference Standard

Two oil and gas well/DEP domain experts should independently code the primary fields, DN, request completeness, difficulty stratum, and refusal mechanism. A third expert should adjudicate. Disagreement should be reported separately rather than reporting only adjudicated labels.

### 8.3 End-to-End Validation of Model B

Real intake should be used to validate teacher-to-proxy transport, exact- and adjacent-bin agreement, bin-boundary uncertainty, proxy-conditioned tail calibration, and subgroup review burden. Teacher-conditioned figures and the actual proxy path must be evaluated separately.

### 8.4 Prospective Human-System Crossover

Four conditions should be compared: unaided review; evidence retrieval only; evidence plus checklist; and the full bounded interface. Endpoints should include review time, missed evidence, expert agreement, override rate, recovery from deliberately incorrect suggestions, confidence calibration, operator contestability, and differential burden.

### 8.5 Criteria for Activating Authority

Every new form of authority should have its own gate. Successful evidence extraction should not automatically activate routing. Successful routing utility should not automatically activate adverse recommendations. Any approval/refusal assistance requires separate legal, engineering, differential-impact, and automation-bias review.

## 9. Reproducibility, Ethics, and Governance

The project preserved source tables, raw API responses, derived artifacts, protocol files, out-of-fold results, and version history. Historical protocols were frozen in local Git before their corresponding runs, but there is no proof that they were deposited in an immutable third-party registry in advance. This paper therefore uses the phrase “version-controlled prospective protocol freezing,” not formal preregistration.

To support independent review, the complete analysis environment and artifact hashes should be frozen; model identifiers, prompts, rendering parameters, and fold assignments should be fixed; and a de-identified data dictionary, model card, intended/prohibited uses, and claim-evidence ledger should be published. Public artifacts should be archived in a repository that provides a DOI. Decision traces are more likely than ordinary outcome tables to contain identities, internal discussions, reasons for exceptions, rule interpretations, and disputes. They therefore require purpose limitation, minimum-necessary collection, role-based access control (RBAC), tiered retention, and disclosure review. The system should not preserve all deliberation forever by default simply because it may “help an agent learn.”

The current record does not contain an IRB determination. Any prospective reviewer/operator study should obtain IRB approval or a non-human-subjects determination before it begins. The system also needs authentication, RBAC, a retention/deletion policy, incident response, field-level audit logs, model/rule version snapshots, and a process for operator correction and appeal.

AI systems participated in extraction, code development, historical reconstruction, internal blind reading, and manuscript drafting. AI cannot be an author or accept responsibility. Human authors must review every analysis, disclose assistance under journal policy, and remain responsible for the final claims.

## 10. Conclusion

Having an outcome database is not the same as having decision data. Historical regulatory archives can allow a model to answer the wrong question accurately, but they cannot uniquely reconstruct the world, actions, and reasons that existed at the time. In this study, an early high score mainly reproduced a circular label; the case-level model identified historical shortfall/difficulty rather than reasonable-effort adequacy; and outcome-state archives incorrectly sent known depth-related refusals into the fast lane. The correct response is not to keep adding features without limits or to let an LMM invent missing reasons. It is to recover traceable fragments, clearly mark what cannot be identified, separate time from construct, and allow failure to remove authority.

The result is not an automated approval system but a bounded architecture for evidence, attention, and trace production. Multimodal services propose source-linked candidates; Model A indicates who should be reviewed first; Model B suggests which historical ruler to use; checklists and rules make missing items and conflicts visible; reviewers verify the sources and remain responsible for final actions; and a prospective ledger preserves rule versions, revision chains, field acceptance, reasons, and overrides. The current prototype can generate a research review package, but it does not yet enforce this complete trace loop or provide prospective validation of utility.

The most important contribution of this paper is therefore not its highest AUC, but a reproducible research discipline: **first ask whether the system preserves a decision trace that can support the judgment, and then ask what the model is qualified to answer; first define the evidence and authority of each component, and then discuss how to combine them.**
